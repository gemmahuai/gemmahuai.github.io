Rubik's Cube

I started playing Rubik's Cube around 2013 when there were very few players, inspired by my friend Changshuo Zhang. In pursuit of faster solving times, I've learned hundreds, even thousands of algorithms, practiced million times, and built close friendships within the Cubing community. Rubik's Cube has profoundly shaped my personal growth, and I'm eager to share it with you.

Personal Records

Here's my profile page in the World Cube Association (WCA) website. College and grad school have been filled with intense academic demands but I'm hoping to practice more and get back to competitions.

Although I couldn't reach the level of world-class Rubik's Cube players, I still find great satisfaction in making even a slight progress. Whether it's mastering a more efficient algorithm, combining steps more smoothly, or creating a novel solution, each small achievement keeps me motivated. For example, I've recently taken up 5x5 speedcubing and have been tracking my solving times with Python. Below is a chart that plots my times against the number of attempts, with running averages overlaid. Watching the overall trend gradually decrease, despite some first-order fluctuations, gives me a sense of accomplishment.

• 3x3 - CFOP

The mainstream advanced 3x3 method is called CFOP, which stands for Cross, F2L (First Two Layers), OLL (Orienting the Last Layer), and PLL (Permuting the Last Layer). Here are some algorithms that I find particularly handy for finger tricks and minimizing rotations.

• 2x2

For 2x2, I primarily use the CLL method along with some tricks from 3x3. While attempting to practice the full EG method, I realized it was similar to learning CFOP, and I wasn't very confident in that learning technique.
